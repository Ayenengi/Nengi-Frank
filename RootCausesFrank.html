Root Causes

Root Cause

Low income communities tend to have a higher rate of health issues due to the lack of accessibility to healthy food, which leads these communities to take matters into their own hands.
  • Lack of resources (i.e. finances or local supermarkets) leads low-income families to eat fast food which leads them to having a higher chance of poor health/health issues.
  • Low income communities/minority communities (like the ones in Philadelphia) coming together to create community gardens where healthy fruits/vegetables are grown and accessible to their communities.
“Black and low-income neighborhoods tend to have higher concentrations of fast-food restaurants and low produce supply stores. Limited access to and consumption of nutrient-rich foods is associated with poor health outcomes.”

(Ashley B. Gripper, et all)
Black and low income communities don't have the same access to big chain supermarkets like the middle class or white families. Without this access they have to result in cheap fast food options. Continuously eating these fast foods leads to health issues.
“...Philadelphia neighborhoods with higher populations of Black people and neighborhoods with lower incomes, on average, tend to have more community gardens and urban farms.”

(Ashley B. Gripper, et all)
Due to not having the same access to these same stores, the Black communities in Philadelphia have started their own community garden so their residents have access to healthy food/ingredients.
Source: Community Solutions to Food Apartheid

Historical Context

United States low-income citizens have always been subjected to food insecurity due to economic and non economic variables.
  • Households may be subjected to food insecurities for various reasons and the government needs resources that can help these families.
  • 8.4 million households are currently facing household insecurity due to significant factors like death and loss of job.
“...approximately 1 in 8 U.S. households experienced food insecurity at some point in the year. Low-income families are at higher risk for food insecurity than other families, and these families may also face higher levels of disruption than other families.”

(Christopher A. Swann)
8.4 million U.S. households are currently experiencing food insecurity for various reasons. It may be due to a loss in job or a death in the family. But this issue of food insecurity has always been an issue in the United States that millions of households face at any given point in the years.
“...[a] household’s recent experience including negative income shocks, moves, and both increases and decreases in household size increase the probability of being food insecure…”

(Christopher A. Swann)
At any given point, a household can have a sudden change in their life that may have them be left without food.
Source: Household history, SNAP participation, and food insecurity
